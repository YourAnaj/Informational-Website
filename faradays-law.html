Faraday’s Law of Induction

Understand the relationship between changing magnetic fields and the induced electromotive force (EMF) in circuits.

What is Faraday’s Law of Induction?

Faraday’s law of induction states that the induced electromotive force (EMF) in a closed circuit is directly proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux through the circuit. In simple terms, when a magnetic field changes near a conductor (like a coil), it induces an electric current.

Applications of Faraday's Law:

Faraday's law is essential in many real-world applications, such as:

  • Transformers: They rely on Faraday’s law to step up or step down voltages.
  • Electric Generators: Rotating a coil within a magnetic field induces an EMF, which powers the grid.
  • Electric Motors: In these devices, a current is induced in the coil which generates motion due to the interaction with the magnetic field.
  • Inductive Charging: Wireless charging for devices such as smartphones uses this principle to transfer energy without physical connectors.

Real-Life Example - The Electric Generator:

In an electric generator, mechanical energy is converted into electrical energy. As the coil rotates in a magnetic field, a change in magnetic flux induces an EMF and creates an electrical current. The greater the number of turns in the coil, the greater the induced EMF. This is how power plants generate electricity for homes and businesses.

Historical Background:

Michael Faraday, an English scientist, first discovered electromagnetic induction in 1831. His experiment involved moving a magnet in and out of a coil of wire, which caused a current to flow. This discovery led to the development of technologies like electric motors, transformers, and generators.
